This proposal develops a data-driven visualization project that explores the datafication of everyday commuting experiences through public transport. Reflecting on making journal experiments, I found the first week’s hand-drawn data visualization particularly meaningful because it presented personal data in a more expressive and human-centered way compared to conventional statistical graphics. Instead of focusing on efficiency and accuracy alone, the visual language allowed emotional and experiential qualities to emerge. This project continues that approach by investigating how routine transport data can be interpreted not only as movement patterns but also as lived experiences.
The project focuses on public transport commuting in Auckland as a form of everyday data that is often recorded but rarely interpreted from a personal perspective. In a future scenario where mobility systems increasingly monitor and optimize travel behavior, commuting data may shape how individuals understand their time, productivity, and environmental impact. Rather than treating transport data purely as infrastructure information, this project explores how such data might reveal hidden rhythms of daily life, including waiting time, unpredictability, emotional responses, and environmental conditions.
The primary data sources will include a combination of existing and self-collected datasets. Existing datasets may include Auckland Transport GTFS timetable data, route frequency information, and weather data collected through public APIs. Self-recorded data will include travel duration, transfer frequency, waiting time, and subjective emotional responses during commuting journeys over a defined period. This dataset will be dynamic and iterative, allowing the visualization to evolve throughout the project. By combining structured transport data with personal observations, the project aims to interpret commuting as both a measurable and experiential activity.
The visualization is planned as an interactive digital map-based interface developed using p5.js or web-based visualization tools. Instead of presenting transport efficiency alone, the visualization will translate commuting patterns into layered visual elements that reflect mood, uncertainty, and environmental context. For example, delays may appear as distortions in route lines, while emotional states may influence color intensity or movement behavior. This approach connects with concepts of everyday datafication discussed in course readings by showing how ordinary activities can be transformed into meaningful narratives through visualization.
The purpose of this visualization is to encourage audiences to reconsider public transport not only as infrastructure but as part of lived experience shaped by time, environment, and emotion. By interacting with the visualization, viewers may recognize how invisible data structures influence daily routines and how personal experiences can be represented through alternative visual languages. Ultimately, the project aims to shift attention from efficiency-centered transport data toward a more human-centered understanding of mobility in future urban life.
