Looking back at my earlier making journal entries, the experiment I found most interesting and meaningful was the Week 1 hand-drawn data visualization. It changed my understanding of what data visualization could be. Before this experiment, I believed that data graphics had to be objective, precise, and computer-generated. However, I discovered that data can also be represented through colour, hand-drawn symbols, and alternative visual languages while still communicating statistical information. Compared with conventional digital charts, this approach is more effective at expressing emotions, relationships, and everyday experiences.
In my next project, I plan to extend this approach by continuing to datafy everyday life experiences using non-traditional visualization methods, especially hand-drawn data graphics. My earlier experiment focused on university students’ daily routines, including commuting time, transport modes, and weekly class time distribution. The Week 1 experiment helped me realise that ordinary daily behaviours can become meaningful sources of data. I chose public transport as my project theme because it is one of the most familiar data environments in my daily life. Since I rely on buses for commuting, this personal connection makes it easier for me to observe and record lived experiences, making it a suitable topic for exploring everyday datafication.
For example, recording interaction time previously allowed me to identify rhythms within a day. This inspired me to further explore commuting time structures, such as waiting time, travel duration, and uncertainty, and how these shape everyday experiences. The project therefore translates personal experiences into perceptible forms of data representation.
In future urban transport systems, commuting behaviour may be continuously recorded and linked with carbon emission monitoring systems. Public transport use could become part of a personal carbon credit framework that encourages sustainable travel choices. This project explores how such data-driven systems might influence people’s understanding of everyday mobility.
The project combines public transport datasets with self-recorded observations in order to represent both system-level temporal structures and individual commuting experiences. Existing datasets include Auckland Transport timetable information (GTFS), route structures, and service frequency, which function as objective static data sources. At the same time, I will collect dynamic subjective data such as waiting time, actual arrival variation, uncertainty, and emotional responses during commuting. Through this combination, data is interpreted not only as transport information but as an expression of everyday experience.
This project connects with course discussions about data as lived experience and experimental visualization approaches beyond conventional statistical graphics. It extends the Week 1 making journal experiment by continuing to explore hand-drawn representations of temporal rhythms and personal data.
The proposed outcome is an emotional commuting trajectory visualization that represents both time variation and affective experience during public transport journeys. For example, delays or moments of urgency may appear as denser or more compressed visual segments, while smoother journeys may appear as stable flows. By integrating objective transport data with subjective emotional records, the visualization encourages audiences to reconsider commuting as a temporal and emotional experience rather than a purely functional activity, revealing hidden structures of waiting, uncertainty, and movement in everyday urban life.
