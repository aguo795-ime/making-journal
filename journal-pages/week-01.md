# Week 01
## Experiment 1 ：Data Drawings

### Group Data Portrait 
### Overview
In this workshop activity, our group produced a collective data portrait based on personal but low-stakes information gathered from each group member. The aim of the exercise was to explore how qualitative and subjective experiences can be translated into a shared visual language without relying on names or direct identifiers.

We designed a short questionnaire and collected anonymous responses using post-it notes. These responses were then translated into a hand-drawn visual composition representing each participant through shapes, colours, and spatial arrangement. The drawing functioned as a collective portrait constructed entirely from data.
### Data Collection
Each group member responded to five short questions designed to capture small aspects of everyday experience. These questions focused on mood, recent activities, attention, and personal routines rather than demographic information.

All responses were written anonymously on individual post-it notes. This allowed the dataset to remain personal while removing direct identification, encouraging interpretation through visual encoding rather than labels.

The collected responses formed the dataset used to construct the collective portrait.

### Visual Encoding Strategy
After collecting the responses, we translated the dataset into a shared visual system.

Different colours represented emotional states reported by participants. Shapes were used to distinguish different individuals, while spatial arrangement across the page suggested relationships between responses. Size variations indicated intensity or duration where relevant.

A legend was included to explain the encoding system so that viewers could interpret the structure of the drawing without additional explanation.

Through this process, the drawing became not only a representation of data but also a visual interpretation of group dynamics.

### Interpretation and Decoding

After completing our portrait, we exchanged drawings with another group and attempted to interpret their dataset.

Without access to their original responses, we relied entirely on their visual encoding system to understand patterns in mood, routines, and experiences across the group. This process highlighted how visual structure can communicate relationships between people even when identities remain anonymous.

At the same time, it also revealed the limits of interpretation when encoding systems are unfamiliar or ambiguous.


### Reflection 
This activity introduced the idea that data visualisation can represent subjective and relational experiences rather than only numerical information. Translating anonymous responses into a visual structure required us to make decisions about how emotional states, routines, and behaviours could be encoded spatially.

One important aspect of the exercise was the process of interpreting another group’s drawing. Without knowing their original dataset, we relied on visual clues to reconstruct possible meanings. This made it clear that data visualisation is not neutral, but shaped by the choices made during encoding.

The activity also reflected ideas from data humanism by treating everyday experiences as meaningful data. Instead of focusing on accuracy or measurement, the drawing emphasised interpretation, ambiguity, and empathy as part of the visualisation process.

### Independent Data Portrait

### Topic
For this exercise, I chose to record how my cat responded to my waking time each morning over the course of one week. I noticed that my cat usually entered my room shortly after I woke up, which made me curious whether her behaviour changed depending on how early or late I got up.

### Data Collection

Each morning, I recorded the time I woke up and observed my cat’s behaviour when she entered my room. I noted the duration of our interaction and the level of urgency in her response.

In all cases, the interaction was initiated by my cat, as she only pushed the door open after noticing movement in the room. If there was no movement, she usually waited quietly outside the door instead.

Across the week, a consistent pattern became visible in the relationship between waking time and interaction duration. Earlier waking times were associated with shorter and calmer interactions, while later waking times resulted in longer interactions and more urgent behaviour, especially when she appeared to be waiting to be fed.

### Visual Encoding Strategy

I translated this data into a horizontal timeline representing each day of the week.

Each interaction is represented by a circle, indicating that the contact was initiated by my cat. The size of each circle reflects the duration of the interaction, with larger circles representing longer contact.

The vertical position of each circle corresponds to my waking time. Earlier waking times appear higher on the page, while later waking times appear lower.

Colour represents the level of urgency in my cat’s behaviour. Blue indicates calm interactions, orange represents slightly more active behaviour, and red marks moments when my cat appeared more urgent in seeking attention or food.

### Reflection

In this exercise, I focused on recording my cat’s behaviour in response to my waking time because this interaction occurs regularly but is usually not consciously observed. By documenting these small daily moments, I was able to recognise a consistent relationship between my waking schedule and my cat’s behaviour.

Through the process of recording the data, I noticed that earlier waking times were associated with shorter and calmer interactions, while later waking times produced longer and more urgent responses. This pattern suggests that my cat’s behaviour is closely connected to feeding expectations and shared daily routines.

Without collecting this data systematically, I would not have recognised how stable this relationship was across the week. The exercise made visible how small repeated interactions structure everyday routines between humans and animals.

In designing the visualisation, I chose to emphasise waking time, duration of interaction, and behavioural urgency. These decisions focused the drawing on temporal patterns rather than spatial movement or sound. As a result, other aspects of the interaction were not included, which reflects the selective nature of data collection.

This exercise relates to ideas from data humanism and the Dear Data project by treating everyday experience as a meaningful dataset. Instead of measuring productivity or efficiency, the drawing represents a relational pattern between myself and my cat, showing how data visualisation can communicate subtle rhythms within daily life.
